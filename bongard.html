Bongard Problems
Bongard problems are a type of abstract logic puzzle that I belive have some intersting properties in the context of knowledge representation. In this document I explain what Bongard problems are and why I think they are interesting.



This is a Bongard problem. There are six things on the left side and six things on the right side. The person who created the problem drew that distinction for a reason. The question is: why?

An answer to this problem is that the things on the left are made of lines and curves, while the things on the right are made of squiggles.

Interesting properties of Bongard problems

A picture is worth a thousand words

... and a Bongard problem is worth twelve-thousand. Bongard problems let us describe easily what we mean by certain words. Here's an exercise: come up with a definition for what the word "triangle" means (in the context of geometry).

I bet your definition was something along the lines of "a triangle is a shape composed of three line segments joined at three corners". Such a definition is just about as good as we can do using just a few pieces of text. But I think that there's a lot of stuff we would consider triangular that wouldn't exactly fit that definition. Take this Bongard problem, for instance:



This problem indicates the difference between triangles and circles. Of the triangles on the left hand side, I think only the top-left and maybe the top-right are well-described by our textual definition of a triangle. Most of the triangles aren't composed of what we would normally describe as "line segments". Moreover, almost none of them are composed of three things at all! One is made of nineteen circles. Another is made of sixteen line segments.

I think we'd have to use a lot more words to come up with a definition of "triangle" that did a satisfying job of indicating everything we mean by that word.

One right answer, multiple equivalent formulations

Most things in life don't have one "right" interpretation---it'd be foolish to assume so; it's not uncommon for people to spend inordinate ammounts of time discussing their different interpretations of the meaning of a movie, article, work of art, book, or any other thing we can interact with.

But Bongard problems seem somewhat different. In a properly crafted problem there always seems only one right answer. There will be, of course, multiple ways of formulating the answer---but all those ways will point at the same thing. We could say, for instance, that the triangle | circle distinction was actually a shape with three sides | shape with one smooth curve distinction. But we'd really be talking about the same underlying idea.

To test this theory, try forming an incompatible interpretation of the triangle | circle problem. The only rule I can come up with is "the things on the left are positioned to the left of the things on the right". This is quite an unsatisfying rule; every single Bongard problem follows it. The rule doesn't do a good job of describing what makes this problem special.

Conjunction

Both of the interesting qualities I've outlined---representing complex ideas and only having one right answer---are not special by themselves. Indeed, we can easily represent complex ideas using text, videos, pictures, and more. There also exist other things that only have one right answer: crossword puzzles, for instance, usually only have one solution.

I think, though, you'd be hard-pressed to find a medium that shares both of these qualities succinctly. We could---and do---create text that accurately describes what we're talking about. But doing so is an extremely tedious and time-intensive process. Creating Bongard problems, on the other hand, is much easier.
